Body Parts You Can Work Out

Major muscle groups are the building blocks of every workout plan. Train each 1–2 times per week for balanced strength and aesthetics.

ChestBench press, push-ups, flyes — pushes forward.
BackRows, pull-ups, pulldowns — pulls toward you.
ShouldersOverhead press, lateral raises — 3 delt heads.
BicepsCurls, chin-ups — elbow flexion.
TricepsDips, pushdowns — elbow extension.
CorePlanks, leg raises — spine stability.
QuadsSquats, lunges, leg press — front thigh.
HamstringsRDLs, leg curls — back of thigh.
GlutesHip thrusts, step-ups — hip power.
CalvesCalf raises — ankle stability.
ForearmsCarries, hangs — grip strength.
HipsLunges, adductor work — lateral movement.

Guide · Workout Types · 7 min read

Types of Workouts

Mix strength, conditioning, and mobility for a body that performs in and out of the gym.

Strength Training

3–6 sets of 5–12 reps with weights, bands, or bodyweight. Builds muscle and bone density.

BarbellsDumbbellsMachinesBodyweight

Cardio (Aerobic)

20–60 min steady effort: running, cycling, swimming, rowing. Strengthens heart and lungs.

HIIT

Short max-effort bursts + rest. Example: 30s sprint, 90s walk × 8 rounds. Time-efficient conditioning.

Circuit Training

4–8 exercises back-to-back. Blends strength and cardio — great when time is tight.

Flexibility & Mobility

Yoga, stretching, foam rolling. Improves range of motion and reduces injury risk.

Plyometrics & Power

Box jumps, med ball throws. Explosive training for athletes after a strength base.

Functional / Athletic

Carries, sled work, agility drills — real-world movement patterns.

Guide · Weekly Splits · 6 min read

How to Organize Your Training Week

Full Body — 2–3 days/week

Best for beginners. Hit all groups each session: squat, push, pull, core.

Upper / Lower — 4 days/week

Mon Upper · Tue Lower · Thu Upper · Fri Lower. More volume per muscle.

Push / Pull / Legs — 3–6 days/week

Push (chest, shoulders, triceps) · Pull (back, biceps) · Legs (quads, hams, glutes).

Guide · Equipment · 5 min read

Gear You Can Train With

  • Bodyweight — push-ups, squats, planks (zero equipment).
  • Resistance bands — portable, joint-friendly, great for travel.
  • Dumbbells & kettlebells — versatile for every muscle group.
  • Barbells & racks — best for heavy progressive strength.
  • Machines & cables — guided paths and constant tension.
  • Cardio machines — treadmill, bike, rower, elliptical.

Guide · Recovery · 5 min read

Rest, Nutrition & Injury Prevention

  • Rest days — 1–2 per week minimum; muscles grow during recovery.
  • Sleep — 7–9 hours nightly for hormone balance and repair.
  • Protein — ~0.7–1 g per lb body weight daily for muscle repair.
  • Hydration — drink water steadily, especially around workouts.
  • Warm-up — 5–10 min light cardio + dynamic stretches before lifting.
Safety: Sharp pain means stop. Learn form first, add weight gradually.

Guide · Getting Started · 4 min read

Your 6-Step Start Plan

  1. Pick a goal — strength, fat loss, endurance, or general health.
  2. Commit to 2–4 training days you can keep every week.
  3. Choose full-body 3×/week if you are new.
  4. Master squat, hinge, push, pull, and plank.
  5. Track reps and weight — progress beats perfection.
  6. Give it 8–12 weeks before judging results.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I work out as a beginner?

Start with 2–3 full-body sessions per week with at least one rest day between. Consistency matters more than daily intensity.

Should I do cardio or weights first?

For building strength, lift first when you are fresh. For endurance races, prioritize cardio. Either order works for general health.

Why am I sore after training?

Delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) is normal 24–48 hours after new exercises. It fades as your body adapts.

Can I work out at home without a gym?

Yes. Bodyweight exercises, bands, and a pair of dumbbells are enough for an effective full-body program.
